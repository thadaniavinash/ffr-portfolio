About Me

My Teaching Journey

I am a full-time faculty member in the Honours Bachelor of Science in Nursing (BScN) program at Georgian College, where I have taught since 2011. My teaching focuses on the biomedical sciences foundational to nursing practice (e.g., Anatomy & Physiology, Pathophysiology) — courses I have both taught and substantially developed from the ground up using open educational resources.

My academic background spans medicinal chemistry (Ph.D., University of Toronto, 2001), post-doctoral research at the University of Chicago, and 5+ years of faculty and research experience at the University of Windsor before joining Georgian. I hold five American patents, published more than 20 peer-reviewed scientific articles (including in prestigious journals such as Nature), and have served as Chair of Georgian College's Research Ethics Board since 2023.

As I enter the FRAME process, I find myself reflecting on a teaching career defined by continual growth, adaptation, and reinvention — from academic researcher to nursing science educator. This reflective process offers me an opportunity to look more intentionally at how my experiences, values, and teaching practices have evolved over time. I remain deeply committed to strengthening my reflective practice in ways that support student success, enrich the learning experience, and contribute meaningfully to program excellence.

Teaching Philosophy

My Teaching Philosophy

My teaching philosophy is grounded in care, connection, and student-centred learning. As a professor in a baccalaureate nursing program, I see teaching as both an academic and relational responsibility. I care deeply about my students, want them to succeed, and strive to do everything within my power to support their growth as novice learners and future registered nurses.

Although I am an introvert by nature, I find great energy and purpose in the classroom. I aim to create a warm, welcoming, and respectful learning environment where students feel seen, supported, and challenged. I believe students learn best when they are actively engaged, so I use case-based learning, discussion, problem-solving, and peer-to-peer collaboration to help them connect theory to nursing practice.

I also believe motivation is one of the most important predictors of student success. At the same time, I recognize that first-year students are still learning how to take responsibility for their own learning journey. My role is to guide them toward confidence, independence, and professional accountability.

As John Dewey is often paraphrased, "We do not learn from experience; we learn from reflecting on experience." This belief shapes my own teaching practice. I continually reflect, adapt, and incorporate useful technologies, including VR and AI tools, to enrich learning and prepare students for contemporary nursing practice.

My teaching philosophy is grounded in care, connection, reflection, and student-centred learning. As a professor in a baccalaureate nursing program at a mid-sized college, I see teaching as more than the delivery of course content. It is a relational, developmental, and professional responsibility. I care deeply about my students, want them to succeed, and strive to do everything within my power to support their academic growth, confidence, and emerging professional identity as future registered nurses.

I approach the classroom with warmth, kindness, and respect. Although I am an introvert by nature, I love being in the classroom because it allows me to connect with students in meaningful ways. I believe that students learn best when they feel psychologically safe, valued, and encouraged to participate. For this reason, I work intentionally to create a welcoming learning environment where students can ask questions, make mistakes, think critically, and gradually build confidence as novice learners.

Because I primarily teach first-year students, I am mindful that many are still developing the habits, discipline, and self-awareness required for success in higher education and in nursing. I believe student motivation is one of the most important criteria for success. However, I also believe that personal responsibility for learning must be learned over time. My role is to support students while also helping them develop accountability, independence, and resilience. I aim to meet students where they are, while guiding them toward the professional expectations of nursing education and practice.

My teaching is strongly rooted in active learning and case-based problem solving. Nursing students need more than factual knowledge; they need to apply concepts, interpret clinical information, make judgments, and communicate their reasoning. I use case studies, classroom discussion, group activities, and applied problem-solving to help students connect foundational knowledge to real-world nursing situations. These strategies encourage students to move from passive reception of information to active engagement with ideas, evidence, and practice-based thinking.

I also value peer-to-peer learning and the development of a classroom community. Students often learn deeply when they explain concepts to one another, compare reasoning, and work through uncertainty together. By building a community of learning, I hope to help students see themselves not only as individual learners, but also as members of a collaborative profession. Nursing is inherently relational and team-based, and the classroom should reflect those values.

Reflection is central to my identity as an educator. As John Dewey is often paraphrased, "We do not learn from experience; we learn from reflecting on experience." I continually reflect on what is working, what is not working, and how my teaching can better support student learning. This reflective practice allows me to adapt my methods, respond to student needs, and remain open to growth as an instructor.

Finally, I am a strong supporter of thoughtful educational technology. As a technophile, I am interested in tools such as virtual reality, simulation, AI chatbots, and other digital resources that can enrich student learning. However, I believe technology should be used purposefully, not simply for novelty. When used well, technology can increase engagement, support practice, provide immediate feedback, and help students visualize complex concepts. My goal is to integrate technology in ways that strengthen learning, deepen understanding, and prepare students for the evolving realities of contemporary nursing practice.

Overall, my teaching philosophy is rooted in the belief that students flourish when they are supported, challenged, actively engaged, and encouraged to reflect on their learning. I aim to be a warm, student-centred, and reflective educator who helps first-year nursing students develop the knowledge, confidence, motivation, and responsibility they need to succeed in their academic journey and future professional practice.
